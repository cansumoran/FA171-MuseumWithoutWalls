Process

In the process of creating my museum without walls, I didn’t have any particular theme or a certain style of artworks that I wanted to curate in mind. One rule that I wanted to stick to was not putting any limitations on the content, media or meaning of artworks that I was collecting. I simply wanted to curate a collection that was diverse, and that I was excited about. Therefore, I let my previous experiences and my research skills take control of the collection process. I searched my galleries to find artworks that I have been lucky enough to experience in person and felt so impressed that I wanted to take a picture or a video to be able to re-visit them. Moreover, I did a thorough search in various online museum collections to discover more artworks that I was interested in.

The collection includes various artworks in various media, from paintings to silverware. The reasons why I was impressed by these artworks are unique to each one of them. However, the attention to detail, the pure creativity and even insanity behind the ideas, the way a message whether it is social, political or economic is delivered in such cleverness, the immersiveness and spiritual feelings they induce were some of the common features of these artworks that attracted me to them.

During this process, all of the artworks I have used were reproductions. One might argue that a reproduction does not hold the place of an original in any type of artwork. This is especially apparent in video/light installations that I have shared as they are aimed to be immersive, to be experienced physically in spaces that are specifically designed for this purpose. Although the experience of viewing a reproduction and an original might not be the same, by using these reproductions, I am able to share some artworks that excite me and I believe should be seen with a vast majority.
